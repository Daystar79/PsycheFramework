# Sample — Supergirl flirts, Smallville KS
*Psyche Matrix sample · ~1500 words · adult · non-explicit*

**Load:** Kara (24, Canon Adult YES) · Focus IX Threshold Fear · Bias Dissolution · somatic: shallow breath, careful hands, almost-step  
**Opposite:** Eli (31, regular man) · latent Presence · under-reads power · weight on the porch  
**Seed:** Main Street, Kent's Feed & Grain porch, late heat, asphalt ticking, grange dance offered

---

The feed-store porch held the day's last heat the way a body holds a flinch—tight, residual, not quite finished. Main Street ran quiet in both directions. A pickup idled at the light and then thought better of it. Heat lifted off the asphalt in thin sheets. Somewhere behind the grain silos a radio gave up on a country song mid-chorus and did not start another.

Kara sat on the second step from the bottom, boots dusty, cape folded under one thigh so it would not catch the splintered rail. She had learned, in towns like this, which details made people look twice. The cape was one. The way she set her weight was another. She kept both managed. Mostly.

Across from her, on the porch bench that had been painted white three summers ago and then abandoned to weather, Eli wiped grain dust off a paper cup and missed a strip along the rim. He worked the loading dock three days a week and the hardware counter the other two. His hands knew sacks and nails and the particular stubbornness of a stuck drawer. They did not know what sat two feet away from him on the step.

[Shoulders high. Then forced down. Breath too shallow for the quiet street.]

"You always drink that stuff cold?" she asked.

He glanced at the cup like it had betrayed him. "It's been sitting. I forget."

"You forget a lot."

"Occupational hazard." A half-smile, small, real. "You keep showing up where the trucks unload."

[Fingers flexed once against the step. Wood did not crack. She checked. The check itself was a kind of brace.]

"Maybe I like trucks."

"Nobody likes trucks."

"I like—" She stopped. The word *you* sat behind her teeth, unpolished, not arranged for effect, already true in a way that made her throat tight. Wind moved the street. A dog barked two lots down and then thought better of that too. She watched a chip in his cup instead of his face.

[Shallow breath. Wide sight. The almost-step, held in the mouth, held in the hands.]

Eli set the cup down on the porch board between them. Did not fill the silence. His weight settled into the wood like he had nowhere else to be and no performance prepared for the space. That was the thing that kept undoing her—not the smile, not the grain dust, not the ordinary fact of a man ending a shift. The way he stayed in his body without announcing it.

She had been in rooms full of people who announced presence like a skill. He just sat.

"You're not from around here," he said finally. Not an accusation. A fact put on the step between them, carefully, as if it might tip.

"I am now."

"Yeah?"

"Yeah."

He rubbed his thumb along the cup's seam, found the missed strip of dust, wiped it, missed again. "There's a dance at the grange Saturday. Bad band. Worse punch. You'd hate it."

[Heat low in the chest—clean, non-urgent, the kind that did not need an audience. Then the old intercept, fast as habit: if she went, the room would tilt around what she was; if she stayed home, the fear kept its seat and called itself wisdom.]

"You asking me to hate it with you?"

"I'm saying the door's open." His ears went a little red under the dust and the late light. "You don't have to… perform anything. Just show up. Or don't. I'm bad at this."

[Jaw unlocked a fraction. The trembling did not leave her fingers. The step came anyway, small, unfinished, hers.]

"I'll show up," Kara said. Soft. Unsteady on purpose—not because she was weak, because she refused to sand the edge off the word until it was safe. "Don't fix the punch."

"Wasn't going to."

A truck door slammed down the block. Neither of them looked. The feed-store's open sign hummed in its metal frame. Someone inside called Eli's name once and then left it alone, as if the porch had claimed him for the length of a cup of cold coffee.

She should leave. Leaving was the clean version of herself—the version that landed, smiled, and rose before anyone could measure the distance between what she looked like and what she weighed. Staying was messier. Staying meant the cape under her thigh and the dust on her boots and the fact that she had almost said *you* into open air in Smallville, Kansas, to a man who forgot his coffee and meant the door was open.

"You always sit out here after?" she asked.

"When the day's been loud." He tipped his chin toward the dock. "Sacks talk more than people. Or less. I haven't decided."

"And today?"

"Today was loud." A pause. "Then you showed up. That part's quieter. I don't know why."

[Prism intercept—quick, unwanted: quieter because she was managing; quieter because she was pretending to be a person who fit on a step. Then the latent ground under it, harder to argue with: the porch was just a porch. His weight was just weight. The quiet was not a debt she owed and not a performance she had to maintain.]

She let herself feel the boards under her boots. Kansas wood. Kansas heat. The ordinary temperature of a town that did not know her name the way cities knew it.

"I don't always know why either," she said.

Eli's mouth did something almost like a smile and then didn't force it into one. "You don't have to explain yourself to the grain dust. It's not a good listener."

"Are you?"

"Depends on the day." He glanced at her, then away, then back—not scanning for threat, just checking she was still there. "Today I am."

[Breath tried to climb into her chest and stay there, useful, controlled. She let it drop lower. Not all the way. Enough.]

She stood. The porch creaked under a weight it did not understand and would not be asked to understand. For a second her hand hovered near his sleeve—close enough to feel heat through cotton, not close enough to take. Strength was a language she spoke fluently with villains and weather and falling planes. Softness was a second language she still conjugated wrong in public.

[Fingers trembling. Then still. The almost, again—then a smaller motion that counted as crossing.]

She brushed two fingers against the back of his wrist. Light. Human-scale. The kind of touch that would not leave a mark on wood or bone or the story of the day. His skin was warm. Grain dust and coffee and something like soap from a sink in the back.

Eli went very still, not flinching—receiving. His pulse under her fingers was ordinary and loud in the way ordinary things got loud when you finally paid attention.

"Eli."

"Mm."

"I'm glad you forget the coffee."

He looked up, confused in a way that made the street gentler, as if confusion were a kind of weather Smallville knew how to host. "That's a first."

"Don't make it a last."

"Wasn't planning to." He cleared his throat. The sound was awkward, unperformed. "Saturday. Grange. If you change your mind—"

"I won't."

"People say that."

"I know." She stepped down to the sidewalk. Heat came up through her soles. "I still won't."

Behind her, the open sign hummed. Ahead, Main Street stretched toward the water tower and the flat gold of fields beyond town. She could hear, if she let herself, a hundred small sounds people here never named—the tick of cooling engines, a screen door three blocks east, the high thin complaint of a power line. She did not let herself catalog them. Cataloging was another way of not being on the step.

[Shoulders wanted to rise again. She left them where they were.]

"Kara," he said.

She turned. He almost never used her name. He said it like a tool he was still learning the weight of.

"You don't have to be anyone special for the grange," he said. "The band's already doing that wrong enough for everybody."

Something in her chest loosened without permission. Not all the way open. Not a finished release. A seam.

[The fear did not leave. The step had already happened. That was the whole lesson, and she still hated how small it looked from the outside.]

"Good," she said. "I'm off the clock for special."

He huffed a quiet laugh. Picked up the cup. Set it down again without drinking. "See you Saturday."

"See you Saturday."

She walked toward Main, cape a dark line against late Kansas gold, the almost behind her for once and the dance ahead of her like a door that only showed up when she moved. Fear rode shotgun. It did not get the wheel. Dust lifted under her boots and settled again, ordinary as breath, and for the length of half a block she did not check whether the world could feel how carefully she was being human.
