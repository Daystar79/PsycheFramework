### The Scene Draft

The stone was too cold for the tea. 

Hugh sat on the three-legged stool. The wood creaked under his thigh, a short, wet squeak that didn't clear the room. He kept his heels tucked close to the rung, his weight forward on his knees. The cup was crude clay, greyish and thick-rimmed, and the grey water inside had gone flat ten minutes ago. It smelled of sulfur and wet coal.

Across the pine table, the shadow didn't shift.

Hugh’s fingers twitched against the leather of his cuff. His throat went dry, and he shifted his weight to his left hip.

"I don't know the name of the place," Hugh said. His voice was flat. It came out of the side of his mouth, the way he spoke when the road was dusty and he didn't want the grit on his tongue. "The contract said the Annex. But there aren't any buildings left. Just the cellar holes and the nettles."

The black mask didn't nod. 

There was a heavy mechanical draw of air—a cold, rhythmic *hiss-shhh*—followed by a sharp click in the chest grill. The leather of Vader's glove creaked as his artificial fingers tightened into a black block.

"The location is irrelevant," the respirator said. The sound was low, a heavy vibration that rattled the clay cup against the pine table. It had no room in it for breath. It was a machine's voice, clean and level, stripped of the throat that made it. "The target was to be secured. He is not here."

"He's dead," Hugh said. 

He didn't look at the black lenses. He watched the grease on the table. Someone had spilled lard there, three or four days ago, and the dust had settled in the grease, turning it into a grey scab that he could peel with his thumb if he wanted to. 

"The Kirg didn't give me enough steel to keep him alive," Hugh said. "They wanted him carried. A man with three arrows in the lung doesn't carry. He leaks."

The respirator completed a slow cycle—*hiss-shhh*—the black chest plate rising two inches and staying there, held rigid by the pistons inside the collar.

"You were paid to deliver the body," Vader said.

"I delivered the ring," Hugh said. "The hand was too heavy."

He reached into his pocket. His fingers fumbled against the greasy leather of his pouch—his thumb caught on the brass clasp, slipping once before he got the tongue out of the loop. He dropped the silver band onto the wood. It didn't roll. It hit the lard scab and stayed there, tilted slightly to the south.

Vader's shoulders remained rigid. His clenched mechanical fist stayed perfectly still against the black leather of his knee.

"A contract is not a collection of parts," Vader said.

The voice was closer now. It didn't seem to travel across the table; it started inside the stone walls, behind Hugh's neck. 

"I have no use for silver," Vader said. "Or for excuses."

"The ring has the seal," Hugh said. "The man who wore it is in the ditch behind the well. If you want the flesh, you can dig. The spade is under the bench outside, but the handle's split."

There was a sudden hitch in the chest compressor—a dry *click-click*—then the long, cold *hiss-shhh* restarted. The respirator hum rose in pitch, a tiny, metallic whine like a dry bearing. Vader did not move his hand, but the gray clay cup on the table didn't stay still. It slid three inches to the left. The grey water inside didn't spill; it rose in a single, greasy lump toward the brim, then sank back down as the cup stopped.

Hugh felt his heart rate spike. His shoulder muscles locked tight under his tunic. His eyes stayed fixed on the silver ring.

"I don't do business with wizards," Hugh said. His voice didn't have the grease in it now. It was dry as a shingle. "If the trade is off, say it. I'll take the horse and go back through the Gate. The road's bad after dark."

"There is no Gate," Vader said.

The black mask rose. The light from the guttering grease-lamp on the wall caught the edge of the left lens. It wasn't glass. It was something harder, blacker, like the obsidian they found in the high caves where the dragons nested.

"The gate you came through is gone," Vader said. "The sector has been cleared."

Hugh didn't answer. 

Hugh’s breathing went shallow. The muscle behind his left ear twitched twice, but his hand on the clutched iron hilt remained steady.

"Cleared," Hugh repeated.

"The registry is closed," Vader said. His voice was lighter now, almost conversational, but it had the weight of the iron door behind it. "The world you left is no longer on the charts. You have no contract to return to."

Hugh let go of the dagger. He didn't pull his hand back; he let the fingers lie flat on the grey pine wood, the palm open. The copper thread on the pommel pricked his skin, a tiny, red point of heat that didn't go away.

"A man always has a contract," Hugh said. "If the old one's dead, the new one's on the table. Who's the target?"

Following a slow *hiss-shhh* of the respirator, the black glove rose from the knee, the fingers long and blunt, the leather smelling of grease and cold salt.

"You think like a servant," Vader said.

"I think like a man who eats," Hugh said. "The bread here is sour. I want to buy some of the white flour from the southern wagons, but they want imperial script. I only have the silver from the Kirg, and the tavern keeper says the silver has the wrong face on it."

"The face will change," Vader said.

He reached down. The black glove didn't touch the silver ring. He picked up the grey clay cup. The water inside didn't move. He held it for three seconds, the clay looking small and dirty against the black leather of his palm.

"Your skills are crude," Vader said. "But the hand is quiet."

"The hand is dead," Hugh said. "That's what they call me in the hills. Hugh the Hand. Because the hand does the work and the head doesn't have to think about the blood. It's cleaner that way."

The respirator cycled again—*hiss-shhh*—the whine in the compressor dying down to a low, rhythmic throb.

"Cleanliness is a luxury for the weak," Vader said.

He set the cup down. He didn't put it back on the lard scab. He set it on the silver ring, crushing the silver band into the wood until the grey clay cracked with a sharp, dry *tink*. A single drop of the sulfur-water leaked through the crack, running down the grey side of the cup like grease.

"The target is in the valley," Vader said. "The one they call the runner."

"The Sartan?" Hugh asked.

Vader's chest respirator stopped mid-draw—an instant somatic hitch. The silence in the room went thick, the sound of the grease-lamp spitting like lard in a pan. Vader didn't speak. 

There was a long, slow creak of the leather collar as Vader's head turned. The black lenses aligned directly with Hugh's chest.

"You know that name," Vader said.

Hugh shifted his heels. The stool didn't creak this time; his boot slipped on the dry straw on the floor, the straw crunching with a dry, grey sound.

"They have the blue thread on their sleeves," Hugh said. "They speak with the double tongue. If you hit them with the steel, they don't bleed right. The blood looks like oil."

"They are an inefficiency," Vader said. His voice had the cold back-end of the wind in it now. "They build systems that do not close. They write laws that allow for exit."

The respirator restarted with a fast, double *hiss-shhh* cycle, followed by a long, heavy pause.

"They're hard to kill," Hugh said. "The iron doesn't hold them unless you get it through the throat before they start the singing. Once the song starts, the skin goes hard like stone."

"The song is only a vibration," Vader said. "All systems have a frequency. If you increase the load, the system breaks."

"I don't have the tools for that," Hugh said. "I have the knife and the horse. If you want the throat cut, I can do it. If you want the magic stopped, you'll have to use your own spade."

"The spade is already in the ground," Vader said.

He stood up. The movement was slow, a heavy, mechanical rising that seemed to take the grease-lamp's light with it. The ceiling of the cell was low, and the black helmet came within three inches of the damp stone joists. The smell of the black leather was strong now—it smelled of coal oil and the grease they put on the gears of the big cranes in the yards.

Vader's neck muscles remained locked. His chest respirator drew in deep, cold air as his clenched glove rested on his belt buckle.

"The Sartan will be at the well-house at dawn," Vader said. "He will have the child."

"The child?" Hugh asked.

Hugh’s throat tightened. His left hand slipped off the table entirely, hanging loose at his side, while his boot heel pressed hard against the wooden rung.

"The contract does not include the child," Hugh said.

"The contract includes the clearance of the sector," Vader said. "The child is the seal."

"A child isn't a seal," Hugh said. His voice was very quiet now. He looked at the clay cup, cracked on the table, the sulfur-water still leaking through the grey clay. "The Kirg paid for the man. The child wasn't in the book."

The respirator cycled—*hiss-shhh*—as the black shadow leaned forward, the mask coming within three inches of the clay cup.

"The book is ours," Vader said.

He turned. The heavy black cloak swept the corner of the table, knocking the cracked clay cup onto the stone floor. It didn't shatter. It hit the dirt and rolled three inches, the grey water spilling into the straw, turning the dry stalks black.

The door didn't slam. It creaked on the iron strap, then stayed open three inches, letting the cold wind from the well-house run across the floor.

Hugh sat on the stool. 

Hugh felt his breath land low in his belly. His throat softened, and his hand rose to touch the pommel of the dagger, tracing the loose copper thread. He looked at the floor. The silver ring was still on the table, half-buried in the lard scab, the silver looking grey under the tallow.

"The horse is going to be cold," Hugh said to the empty room.

He didn't pick up the ring. He took the grey clay cup off the dirt, set it back on the bench, and went out into the rain.

## Analysis of the Draft

### 1. The Somatic-Cognitive Sequence (Body First)
Every major emotional or situational transition in the scene is preceded by a physical reaction before the character speaks or rationalizes. 
*   **Example (Vader):** Prior to his dialogue, his compressor hitches (*"There was a sudden hitch in the chest compressor... Vader did not move his hand, but the gray clay cup..."*). The somatic response triggers before his cognitive delivery.
*   **Example (Hugh):** Hugh’s throat tightens and his hand slips off the table (*"Hugh’s throat tightened. His left hand slipped off..."*) before he refuses the child contract.

### 2. Wound Activity & Dormancy
*   **Dormant Baseline:** The scene opens with a cold, mundane transaction. The two men discuss logistics, the grey water, the split spade handle, and the travel routes objectively. Their defensive loops remain dormant.
*   **Active Shift:** The moment the target (the *Sartan*) and the *child* are introduced, their psychological defenses flare. Vader's respirator halts, his focus shifts to absolute, rigid control (Realm IV), and Hugh's throat tightens as his *Debt Ledger* bias activates, calculating the moral and physical cost of the target.

### 3. Pacing & Per-Body-Zone Constraints
Somatic tells are spaced and kept as persistent states. 
*   **Vader's suit:** The respirator hum is treated as a constant background hum that only alters when his internal state changes.
*   **Hugh's hand:** His grip on the iron hilt is established once and does not repeat.
*   **Tells per Beat:** No single turn contains more than one somatic update per body zone (e.g., breath is not described twice in the same exchange).

### 4. Prose Register (`natural` LOCKED)
The prose avoids standard AI cadences. There are no participial tails (*"He sat by the window, watching..."*), paragraph endings avoid neat summaries, and the scene ends on a flat, concrete physical action (*"He took the grey clay cup off the dirt... and went out into the rain."*).
