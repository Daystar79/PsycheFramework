# Natural Prose Directive
*Optional style pack — Natural / asymmetric (house) · not the session default*

This document outlines rules for avoiding "perfect pattern spaces" in AI-generated prose: jagged rhythm, cognitive friction, anti-synthesis. It is the **house natural pack** (Anthony/Barker-adjacent lane).

## 1. Syntactical Asymmetry & Broken Meter

AI models tend to generate balanced, rhythmically symmetrical sentences (e.g., alternating between short and medium sentences, or ending paragraphs on a neat poetic cadence). Human prose is jagged and irregular.

### Rules:
- **Ban the Participial Tail:** Avoid the recurring AI sentence pattern of `[Action] + [Participle Phrase]` (e.g., *"He stood by the window, watching the rain, thinking of the other."*). Replace with separate, active verbs or direct actions (e.g., *"He stood by the window. He watched the rain and thought of the other."*).
- **Abrupt Syntax Swings:** Use run-on sentences to mimic a racing or obsessive mind, and follow them with single-word or short fragments. Do not smooth out the rhythm.
- **Weak Ends:** Let sentences occasionally end on flat, weak, or conversational words (*"too,"* *"instead,"* *"then,"* *"with"*), rather than always landing on a poetic, heavy beat.
- **Asymmetric Paragraph Lengths:** Do not write neatly balanced paragraphs of identical sizes. Let a massive, dense block of sensory weight be followed by a single-sentence paragraph, then a medium one.

## 2. Dialogue Drift (Conversational Asymmetry)

AI characters engage in perfect "ping-pong" dialogue, where each line directly acknowledges and replies to the previous line. Real human speech is fragmented, oblique, and preoccupied.

### Rules:
- **Talking Past:** Characters should frequently ignore questions, respond to their own internal thoughts, or change the subject entirely. 
- **The Oblique Answer:** Respond to the *subtext* or the emotional pressure of a statement rather than the literal words spoken.
- **Trailing & Interruption:** Real dialogue is messy. Characters start sentences, lose the thread, get distracted, trail off, or cut each other off mid-word.
- **Non-Sequiturs:** Allow characters to bring up completely unrelated physical observations or operational details in the middle of an emotional beat.
- **The Vernacular Baseline (No Clinical Speeches):** Characters must speak in their organic, everyday idiolect. Banish dry clinical analysis, academic parsing, or formal speech-making unless a character is reading a document aloud. Use plain, direct, visceral words.

## 3. Irregular Sensory Focus (The Asymmetric Eye)

AI tends to write "checklist descriptions," systematically listing sights, sounds, smells, and textures in a balanced sweep. Humans experience sensory inputs through the filter of their current obsession, anxiety, or physical state.

### Rules:
- **Single-Focus Drift:** In high-tension or intimate scenes, do not describe the whole room. Focus entirely on one or two narrow, sometimes irrelevant details (e.g., the texture of a thumb rubbing a seam, the smell of grease on a cuff) and ignore the rest of the setting.
- **Atmospheric White Noise:** Describe details that lead nowhere. A human writer notices a dead insect on a sill, a chip in the ceramic, or a dog barking down the road, and then moves on. Do not turn every detail into a metaphor or a plot point.
- **Dynamic Blindness:** If a character is panicked or highly focused, they should be temporarily blind to major environmental elements (e.g., not noticing someone entering the room, or failing to register a drop in temperature).
- **Concrete Visuals vs. Mathematical Abstractions:** Ban abstract, mathematical, geometric, or clinical words when describing physical environments, settings, or physical postures (e.g., avoid *"geometry,"* *"dimension,"* *"trajectory,"* *"symmetry,"* *"equilibrium"*). Always translate these into concrete, visceral words (e.g., use *"the shape of the room"* instead of *"the geometry of the room"*; use *"how her neck bent"* instead of *"the angle of her neck"*).

## 4. Physical Friction & Clumsiness

AI characters move like dancers—performing sequences of actions with perfect efficiency, grace, and coordination. Real humans are clumsy, physically limited, and constantly reacting to minor physical resistance.

### Rules:
- **The Fumble Rule:** Characters should fumble with keys, trip slightly on thresholds, drop pens, misjudge distances, adjust their clothes because they are pinching, or have to tug twice on a sticky drawer.
- **Biological Realism:** Bodies require maintenance. Characters clear their throats, stretch stiff limbs, rub sore muscles, wipe sweat from their foreheads, or squint against glare.
- **Vestment Resistance:** Keep clothing barriers active and awkward. Robes catch on furniture, fabric scratches, boots are stiff and hard to pull off, and sleeves have to be rolled up repeatedly because they slip.

## 5. Cognitive Drift & Memory Imperfection

AI possesses perfect recall of all preceding story events, spatial layouts, and conversation history. Human characters operate with limited, flawed, and biased minds.

### Rules:
- **Memory Drift:** Characters should occasionally misremember a detail (e.g., forgetting where they set down a mug, or misrecalling the color of a vehicle).
- **Losing the Thread:** Characters mid-thought or mid-sentence should occasionally get distracted by a sudden noise or a somatic spike and lose their train of thought.
- **Biased Interpretation:** Characters do not interpret expressions or tones with objective accuracy. They misread looks, project their own anxieties onto others, and make incorrect assumptions that remain uncorrected.

---

## 6. Lexical Sticking (Repetitive Fixations)

AI goes to great lengths to avoid repeating words, using a wide vocabulary of synonyms to maintain variety. Human minds (and close-POV prose) get "stuck" on specific words that mirror their obsessions.

### Rules:
- **Obsessive Repetition:** If a character is cold, let the word *"cold"* or *"chill"* repeat naturally three or four times on a page. Do not artificially swap in *"frigid,"* *"drafty,"* or *"gelid"* just for the sake of variety.
- **Idiolect Lock:** Characters have preferred words, phrases, and speech cadences that they reuse. Do not diversify their vocabulary beyond what their background (e.g., a specific professional, technical, or regional dialect) would naturally yield.

---

## 7. The Anti-Synthesis Rule (No Narrative Closure)

AI paragraph structure frequently ends with a neat summary sentence that resolves or packages the emotional state (e.g., *"It was a silent pact they both understood,"* or *"The weight of the choice settled over him like a shroud."*).

### Rules:
- **No Synthesized Summaries:** End paragraphs on raw, concrete physical facts, actions, or unanswered dialogue. Let the reader assemble the emotional takeaway.
- **Unresolved Cuts:** Leave scenes on a jagged edge. When a scene cuts (`---`), do not tie the moment up with a neat bow. Let it end in the middle of a breath, an unfinished task, or a silent realization.
- **Watch the Single-Word Kicker:** A short closing beat ("Enough." "Then." "Nothing.") can slide into a packaged takeaway even without a full sentence. Test it: if the word is doing *interpretive* work (telling the reader what the moment meant) rather than *descriptive* work (the last concrete thing that happened), cut or replace it.

---

## 8. Beat Non-Recursion (Cross-Scene Repetition)

Rule 6 licenses word-level repetition *within* a scene or character's idiolect — that's a feature. It is not license to reuse the same *structural sequence* across separate scenes. AI models that find a rupture/collapse/breakdown sequence that "worked" will quietly redeploy the same shape (same trigger → same failure point → same syntax collapse → same closing image) for the same character later in the manuscript. This reads as a lack of imagination even when no single sentence is copied.

### Rules:
- **No Duplicate Collapse Shapes:** A character's breakdown, rupture, or loss-of-control sequence must not reuse the same *ordered beats* twice (e.g., register fails → repeated broken word → "no sentence left" → name spoken without syntax → closing simile). Vary which system fails first each time — voice, posture, breath, vision, memory — even if a signature verbal tic repeats.
- **Pre-Write Check:** Before drafting any rupture/breakdown scene, scan prior instances for that character. If the shape matches an earlier one closely, restructure the *order and mechanism* of the collapse, not just the wording.
- **Distinguish Idiolect from Template:** A recurring word or phrase tied to a character's background or fixation (Rule 6) is allowed to reappear. A recurring *scene architecture* is not.
